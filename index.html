02 Selected Research
SSRN · 2026 · Economic Theory
Household Infrastructure Stabilization as a Structural Economic Resilience Mechanism: A Framework for Distributed Infrastructure and Energy Systems
This paper introduces a conceptual framework examining whether integration between housing infrastructure and localized energy production may function as a structural stabilizing mechanism within modern economies... This paper introduces a conceptual framework examining whether integration between housing infrastructure and localized energy production may function as a structural stabilizing mechanism within modern economies. Household financial fragility is often influenced by volatility in essential expenditures, particularly housing and energy costs, which can act as channels for broader economic shock transmission. The framework explores whether reducing exposure to such volatility through infrastructure design could contribute to more stable household balance sheets and in turn enhance macroeconomic resilience. Using a circulation-based perspective, the paper describes a "stability flywheel" in which improved predictability at the household level supports more consistent participation in local economic activity, reinforces community-level resilience and reduces amplification of downturns. It also considers potential interactions between distributed residential energy systems and evolving grid demands in regions experiencing growth in electricity-intensive infrastructure. The objective is not to prescribe specific policies, but to outline a set of mechanisms and research questions that may inform further empirical and modeling work at the intersection of household finance, infrastructure economics and financial stability.

03 About

I'm a systems builder focused on uncovering how complex structures actually behave and where they fail.

My work sits at the intersection of infrastructure, systems, economics and intelligence. Technically, I operate from the ground up. I'm comfortable at the hardware, firmware, OS level and application layer. I build into distributed systems, data pipelines and AI-assisted tooling. I prioritize control, observability and resilience, favoring hardened, minimal environments over abstraction-heavy approaches.

I don't treat domains as isolated. The same structural patterns show up in code, markets, networks and human systems. My work is identifying those patterns, modeling them and turning them into something measurable and actionable.

I build to understand and once understood, to reshape.
